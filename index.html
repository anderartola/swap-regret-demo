What's going on

Both algorithms face the same stream of payoff vectors u¹, u², ... that you generate by clicking. In each round, each algorithm commits to a distribution over the two actions before seeing u, then observes the full payoff vector.

EW (Exponential Weights) keeps cumulative payoffs per action and plays each action with probability proportional to (1+ε) raised to its cumulative payoff. It guarantees vanishing external regret: no single fixed action would have done much better in hindsight.

SDA (Stationary Delegation Algorithm) runs k internal EW experts, one per action. Each round, expert a recommends a distribution α̃ₐ. SDA stacks these as rows of a row-stochastic matrix and plays the stationary distribution α of that matrix, satisfying α = α·Ã. Expert a then receives the scaled payoff vector αₐ·u. This guarantees vanishing swap regret: no rerouting a → f(a) of your own actions would have done much better.

Swap regret is strictly stronger than external regret. The orange dashed line shows SDA's external regret, which is bounded by its swap regret — so it also vanishes, just often more slowly than pure EW because each SDA expert only updates on its own weighted slice of history.

Things to try

Stationary adversary: click repeatedly near (0.8, 0.2). Both algorithms converge to "play action 1" and regrets stay near zero.

Phase change: click (1, 0) many times, then switch to (0, 1) many times. Watch how quickly each algorithm adapts.

Alternating payoffs: alternate between (1, 0) and (0, 1). Look at how the two SDA experts develop different views of history (shown in the detail line under SDA's bars).

Learning rate sweep: set ε to 0.05 (very cautious) or 1.0 (very aggressive) and replay the same sequence. Large ε learns faster but reacts more to noise; small ε is the opposite.
